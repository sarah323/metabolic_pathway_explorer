Module 7 — High-Yield Summary

Key concepts, clinical pearls, and memory aids for examination revision.

🔑 Key Concepts

  • Insulin → IR → IRS1 → PI3K → AKT is the core metabolic cascade.
  • AKT suppresses FOXO1 (gluconeogenesis OFF) and activates mTORC1 → SREBP‑1c (lipogenesis ON).
  • AKT inhibits HSL (lipolysis OFF) and activates LPL (TG clearance).
  • AS160 phosphorylation by AKT enables GLUT4 translocation.

💡 Clinical Pearls

  • Insulinoma: Inappropriately elevated insulin + C‑peptide during hypoglycaemia.
  • DKA: Absolute insulin deficiency → HSL unrestrained → massive lipolysis → ketone body production → metabolic acidosis.
  • T2DM: Selective hepatic insulin resistance — FOXO1 stays active (gluconeogenesis continues) while SREBP‑1c is paradoxically active (VLDL overproduction).

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

  • Confusing C‑peptide levels: elevated in insulinoma & early T2DM; low/absent in DKA & exogenous insulin use.
  • Forgetting that FOXO1 is active when AKT is off — driving hepatic glucose output.
  • Overlooking that mTORC1‑SREBP‑1c can be active even when the rest of the pathway is resistant (selective resistance).

🧠 Memory Tips

  • "AKT kicks FOXO out" — AKT phosphorylates FOXO1 → nuclear exclusion → GNG OFF.
  • "No insulin = No LPL, Lots of HSL" — DKA features lipaemic serum from unchecked lipolysis.
  • "IR → IRS → PI3K → AKT → AS160 → GLUT4" — the glucose uptake chain.
📋 High-Yield Comparison: In DKA, insulin is absent → FOXO1 active, SREBP‑1c off, HSL fully active. In T2DM, insulin is high but sensitivity is low → FOXO1 remains active (hepatic glucose output), yet mTORC1‑SREBP‑1c paradoxically drives lipogenesis. This explains the atherogenic dyslipidaemia characteristic of T2DM.

About the Metabolic Simulator

Purpose

This interactive educational simulator provides a dynamic environment for exploring insulin signalling, metabolic regulation, and disease pathophysiology. Rather than memorizing pathways, learners visualize how insulin concentration and tissue sensitivity influence intracellular signalling, enzyme regulation, laboratory findings, and clinical disease states.

Target Audience

  • Medical students
  • Biomedical sciences students
  • Pharmacy students
  • Medical laboratory sciences students
  • Postgraduate trainees
  • Medical educators

Learning Objectives

After completing this module, learners should be able to:

  • Explain insulin receptor signalling.
  • Describe PI3K-AKT activation.
  • Explain GLUT4 translocation.
  • Interpret FOXO1 regulation.
  • Explain mTORC1 signalling.
  • Describe SREBP‑1c mediated lipogenesis.
  • Explain regulation of HSL and LPL.
  • Interpret laboratory findings.
  • Differentiate insulinoma, DKA, and type 2 diabetes.
  • Apply biochemical principles to clinical cases.
